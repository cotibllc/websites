Every leadership framework you were ever trained on was built for humans.

Servant leadership. Situational leadership. Transformational leadership. Every model assumes the people you lead are observable, can clarify intent through conversation, and are accountable in the ways humans are accountable. That assumption made sense when your entire workforce was human. It is already breaking down, and most organizations have not noticed yet.

In the latest episode of the IT XP, I bring my Tech Lead Shift leadership series to the podcast for the first time. The episode is rooted in the first article I published on the Substack, [The Leadership Gap Nobody Is Talking About](https://techleadshift.substack.com/p/the-leadership-gap-nobody-is-talking), and it covers something I have been sitting with since ChatGPT dropped to the masses in December 2022.

**The room that didn't know what it didn't know**

It started in a steering committee meeting. A head of engineering was running a demo for a new coding agent. The projected time savings were solid. The room was nodding. Then somebody asked the natural follow-up about security holes and architecture drift. The silence that followed was short but telling. The answer came back: "We'll monitor it."

Nobody asked what monitoring actually meant for a system that runs 24 hours a day, does not attend standups, and has no personal incentive to flag its own mistakes.

That moment is not really about the tool. It is about the gap between how fast organizations adopt technology and how slowly the leadership thinking is catching up. The pattern I keep seeing is that companies treat AI adoption like any other tech refresh. Capability, cost, integration. Same playbooks they have always used for human teams. And then the assumption gets made that once the model is in production, the existing management layers will simply absorb the new workload.

Except the new workload is not human.

**Where the frameworks fall apart**

I walk through three of the most commonly taught leadership models in the episode and show exactly where each one breaks.

Servant leadership is built on the idea that leaders exist to remove obstacles so their people can do their best work. That works when your team has egos, career goals, and emotional needs. Now replace a team member with an autonomous coding agent. It has no ego. It does not care about career progression. It will generate output that meets the letter of the prompt even if the spirit violates your internal standards. What the agent actually needs is a tightly defined objective, strict guardrails, and continuous oversight of its outputs. Servant leadership does not tell you how to provide any of that.

Situational leadership says you adapt your style based on the maturity of the individual. Directing. Coaching. Supporting. Delegating. The problem is an AI agent does not mature. It does not move along that continuum. Its maturity is whatever version of the model you deployed last week, plus whatever prompt engineering you applied last Tuesday. The entire diagnostic framework collapses because there is no individual to assess.

Transformational leadership is about inspiring vision, intellectual stimulation, and individualized consideration. Powerful stuff for humans motivated by purpose and personal growth. An AI agent does not respond to vision. It responds to parameters. It does not get intellectually stimulated. It executes whatever inference path its weights dictate. Trying to inspire it is like trying to inspire your laptop.

The frameworks are not broken. They are aimed at the wrong target.

**The part that should make you uncomfortable**

Here is what I think most organizations are not seeing yet. When you introduce AI agents, you are not adding neutral productivity tools. You are adding amplifiers. They inherit whatever culture already exists and scale it at machine speed.

If your organization has vague requirements, you are going to get vague, inconsistent agent results at volume. If you reward activity over outcomes, you are going to get mountains of low-value work that nobody has time to review. If your culture avoids hard accountability conversations, those conversations become even harder when the failure involves something that is not human.

I talked to my direct manager about this the day I recorded this episode. At the pace things are moving, something is going to break. And when that AI agent is responsible for the failure, the first question is going to be who is responsible for the AI agent. Right now, in most organizations, nobody has a clean answer to that.

**The question to sit with**

I close the episode with the same question I close the article with. If your current leadership framework assumes a fully human workforce, and you are about to add a non-human one that scales whatever culture you already have, what parts of how you lead today are quietly broken and you just have not seen it yet?

You do not need to answer it now. I am not answering it in this episode either. But you should be thinking about it before your next deployment.
