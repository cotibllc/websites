## My Current AI Stack

I've been transparent about the fact that I'm AI-first now. But people always ask *which* tools, and *for what*. So I laid it all out:

- **Claude** — my primary thinking and writing partner. Long-form reasoning, episode strategy, content structure.
- **ChatGPT** — still solid for certain tasks, good for quick iterations and anything where I want a second opinion against Claude.
- **Gemini** — I use it selectively, but Google's integration play is hard to ignore long-term.
- **Grok** — surprisingly useful for real-time context and anything adjacent to what's happening on X.
- **ElevenLabs** — this one changed my production workflow. Audio quality, music generation, and voice work that would've taken me hours now takes minutes.

The honest take: no single tool does everything. The 70/30 rule still applies — AI handles the heavy lifting, but my 25+ years of IT experience is what makes the output actually useful. That judgment layer isn't replaceable.

## 12 Days in China: What Actually Hit Different

I'm not going to pretend I came back with some grand geopolitical thesis. What I came back with were *observations* — and some of them were genuinely eye-opening for someone who thinks about technology adoption for a living.

**Cashless is not a trend there. It's infrastructure.** WeChat Pay and Alipay aren't apps people use sometimes. They're how society *functions*. I watched transactions happen in places where I'd expect cash to be the only option. It reframed how I think about what "digital transformation" actually looks like when it's fully baked in.

**EVs everywhere.** Not Tesla-everywhere. *Chinese EV brands* everywhere. The adoption curve is different. The price points are different. The charging infrastructure in urban areas is ahead of what I've seen in most U.S. cities.

**Mobile-first isn't a design principle — it's a worldview.** Everything from navigation to ordering food to accessing services runs through a phone in a way that makes U.S. mobile adoption look incremental by comparison.

I'm bringing some of these observations into future episodes because there are real implications for IT professionals thinking about global infrastructure, vendor diversification, and what "modern" actually means.

## Why I'm Not Getting a Doctorate

I'll be direct about this one.

I spent real time thinking about going back for a doctoral program. The credentialing appeal is obvious — in IT leadership circles, letters after your name carry weight. But when I pressure-tested it against what I actually want to build, it didn't survive the analysis.

Instead, I'm going all-in on a **LinkedIn and Substack leadership article series** under the Corporate Hardcore umbrella. Structured, sourced, built on real-world experience. The kind of content that practitioners actually read and use — not academic output that lives behind a paywall and gets cited twice.

This isn't a consolation prize. It's a deliberate choice. The audience I want to reach isn't in a PhD program. They're running IT departments, managing hybrid teams, and trying to figure out how AI fits into their organizations without losing their heads. I can serve that audience directly.
