date: "2025-12-15"

I arrived at five-fifteen.

The venue had been decorated. There were balloons. A DJ was playing something that was almost music. Someone had put together a slideshow of "team highlights" running on a screen near the entrance, a loop of people in conference rooms pointing at whiteboards.

HR Jill found me within four minutes. She always finds me within four minutes. "Chuck, so glad you made it. Having fun?"

"Yes," I said. We both understood the social contract.

The CEO gave a speech around five-forty-five. Record growth. Best year in a decade. Amazing team. He said "team" eleven times. I counted, because it was something to do with my hands.

He did not mention the Q3 restructuring. He did not mention the headcount reduction or the people who weren't at the party because they no longer worked there. He mentioned the record growth twice more and then thanked everyone for their dedication and sacrifice, and I thought: that word, sacrifice, doing a lot of work in that sentence.

Three weeks earlier, I'd been told I was a solid three. Not because of my performance, my manager explained, but because of factors, complex ones, systemic ones, budgetary ones. I'd spent forty-seven minutes on a self-assessment explaining my value and then forty-eight more on a review where someone read my rating off a document they'd already finished.

Now I was holding a paper plate with a piece of focaccia on it, listening to a speech about our best year ever.

There's a word for this. Overcompensation. The party gets louder when the year gets harder. The enthusiasm scales inversely with the truth. It's a survival tactic, for the company. If everyone celebrates together, the celebration becomes the story.

I checked my watch at six-oh-two. Forty-seven minutes. Long enough to be seen. Short enough to preserve dignity.

I put my plate on a table near the door and got my coat.
