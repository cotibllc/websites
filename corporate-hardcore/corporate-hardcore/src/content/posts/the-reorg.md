The org chart arrived this morning.

Subject line: "Exciting Organizational Updates." I've received this email six times in eighteen years. Different sender each time. Same words. "Streamlining operations." "Optimizing our structure." The chart attached as a PDF.

My box moved two inches to the right. Same level. Same responsibilities that were never clearly defined in the first place. Same manager, who reports to the same person, who reports to someone else. I counted the layers between me and actual decision-making authority. The number hasn't changed.

Three departments merged into one. The new department has the same number of people doing the same work. We were Business Solutions. Now we're Strategic Operations. Both names mean nothing specific. I've had time to think about that.

We had a meeting to discuss the chart. Forty-seven minutes. Twelve spent looking at the chart, thirty-five spent discussing what it means going forward. Someone asked about headcount. "We're evaluating opportunities." Someone asked about budget. "Still being determined." Someone asked about timeline. "We'll circle back on that."

I wrote those answers down. The notebook page looked familiar.

Implementation began immediately, which meant everyone kept doing exactly what they were doing before. New email signatures. Updated company directory. Two people changed offices, third floor to third floor, different cubicles, same view of the parking lot. The move required IT support tickets. I processed those tickets.

A new SharePoint site was created for the reorganized department. It contains the same documents as the old one. Someone spent four hours building the new site. Someone else spent two hours migrating the files. The old site still exists.

Three months from now, nothing will operate differently. The same people will make the same decisions through the same informal channels they used before the chart arrived. The formal structure will remain beside the point, the way it always has been.

I know this because I've watched it six times. The seventh looks exactly like the others.

What I've started to notice is that the reorg is never really about the structure. The structure is the part they can move on a slide. The actual work, how decisions get made, who has context, which systems talk to each other, lives somewhere the chart doesn't show. You can redraw the boxes. The work stays where it was.

We'll get a survey about the reorganization in a few weeks. It will ask if I feel more aligned with strategic objectives.

I'll select neutral. That seems accurate.
