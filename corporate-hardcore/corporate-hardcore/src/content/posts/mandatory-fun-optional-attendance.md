The invitation said "You're Invited."

Exclamation point. "Invited" in all caps.

I read the body copy. "Join us for an evening of celebration and team building." Two sentences down: "Attendance will be tracked."

So not invited. Required.

There was an RSVP deadline. A dietary restrictions form. A note that team photos would be taken. And a line at the bottom explaining that Secret Santa participation was "strongly encouraged."

Strongly encouraged means mandatory. Just with better branding.

I've been to enough of these to understand what they're actually for. The holiday party isn't a reward. It's a ritual. It's the moment where the company asks you to celebrate together, to perform gratitude for the opportunity to work there, to stand in a rented event space with a paper plate and act like the year was good.

Even if the year wasn't good.

Even if there were layoffs in Q3. Even if you just finished forty-seven questions explaining why you deserve to keep your job. Even if you got your review back with "meets expectations" and a cost adjustment that doesn't cover the increase in your health premium.

Still, come celebrate. Dress is festive business casual.

I clicked "Accept." Not because I wanted to go. Because declining requires a meeting with HR to discuss team alignment.

Last year, someone asked if attendance was actually mandatory. HR said, "Of course not. But we'd love to see everyone there." That person was part of the Q2 restructuring.

I've been going to the holiday party for eighteen years. I arrive at five-fifteen. I leave at six-oh-two. Forty-seven minutes. Long enough to be seen. Short enough to avoid karaoke.

The system doesn't ask you to enjoy it. It just asks you to show up.
