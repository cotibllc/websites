---
title: "The New Colleague"
date: 2026-04-01
description: "A new team member joined this week. Doesn't have a desk. Already has calendar access. It scheduled three meetings before anyone knew its name."
arc: "AI as Employee"
---

A new colleague joined the team this week.

No onboarding. No introduction email. No first-day awkwardness where someone shows them where the printer is. They just appeared in the system on Monday with full calendar access, and by Tuesday morning there were three new meetings on my schedule that nobody had asked for.

The invite subject lines were confident. "Alignment Sync — Infrastructure Priorities." "Weekly Velocity Check-In." "Cross-functional Standup: Q2 Initiatives." I looked at the attendee list. Twelve people across four departments. Nobody had agreed to attend. Nobody had been consulted. The meetings were simply there, the way weather is there, something that arrived without negotiation.

I asked my manager who approved the calendar integration.

He said it was rolled out by IT last week as part of the productivity suite upgrade. He seemed mildly surprised I was asking. The capability had been enabled; therefore, the meetings were legitimate. That was the full logic chain.

This is what I've started thinking of as the first AI leadership problem, and it isn't technical.

The technical part was straightforward. Someone provisioned an AI scheduling tool. It was given access. It did what it was designed to do. The meetings appeared on calendars because calendars is what the tool had access to. Everything worked exactly as built.

The problem is that nobody asked the question that should have come before the provisioning: what does this tool have permission to decide on its own, and what requires a human to make the call? That's not a configuration question. It's a leadership question. And it didn't get asked, because the upgrade happened through IT's normal deployment process, which doesn't have a step for "consider what the software is now authorized to do on behalf of your organization."

The meetings have been running for two weeks. I attended the first one. Eight people in a video call, nobody sure why they were there, the agenda a list of topics the AI had assembled from recent email threads. We spent twenty-two minutes establishing that the meeting had no clear owner, no decision to make, and no reason to recur. Then we blocked another thirty minutes to discuss whether to cancel the series.

The new colleague was not in the meeting. It doesn't attend the meetings it creates. It just makes them.

I'm documenting this the way I document everything: not because I think it will change anything immediately, but because the pattern is worth naming before it becomes background noise. An AI agent was given access to a shared resource, calendars, without a defined boundary for what it was allowed to do with that access. The result was a series of obligations nobody agreed to and everyone has to spend time undoing.

That's not a productivity problem. That's a management problem. And the management gap wasn't created by the AI.

It was already there. The AI just made it visible.
