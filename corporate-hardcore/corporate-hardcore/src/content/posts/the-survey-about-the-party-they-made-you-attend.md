Monday morning. Subject line: "Thank you for an amazing night, and a quick favor!"

I opened it.

HR was thanking everyone for their "enthusiastic participation" in the holiday celebration. Attached was a fifteen-question feedback survey. They wanted to know if the venue was appropriate, if the food met our dietary needs, if the entertainment was engaging, if we felt the event "reflected company values."

At the bottom, there were photos from the event. Candid shots. People at the buffet. The CEO mid-speech, gesturing at something optimistic. And one of me, standing near the snack table, paper plate in hand, staring at nothing.

I looked at that photo for a while.

The survey had a five-day response window. There was a note at the bottom: "Your feedback helps us improve next year's celebration." Next year's. Already.

Fifteen questions about a party I attended for forty-seven minutes under the implicit threat of a conversation about team alignment.

This is the part that gets me, not the party, not even the mandatory attendance. It's the belief, or the performance of belief, that measuring it will improve it. That if we just gather enough data about the celebration, we can optimize the gratitude. That the reason people weren't enthusiastic enough wasn't the circumstance that required the party in the first place, it was the venue, or the DJ, or the ratio of passed appetizers to buffet stations.

The system never stops. Not even for the party. Especially not for the party.

I filled out the survey. Four out of five on venue, three on entertainment, five on food because the focaccia was actually good. On the open-ended question, "What would make next year's event even better?", I typed "nothing" and then deleted it and typed "the current format works well."

Same goal. Better packaging.

I hit submit and opened my calendar. Q1 planning cycle starts in two weeks.
