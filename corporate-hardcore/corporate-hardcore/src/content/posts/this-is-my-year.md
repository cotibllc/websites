My coworker stopped by my desk this morning.

"Chuck," they said, "this is my year. I can feel it."

They've been here six years. Every January, it's their year. By March, it's just another year. I've seen this enough times to know the trajectory without asking.

They listed their goals. Promotion track. Side hustle. Morning routine. Starting the gym again, but for real this time. Learning Portuguese. All of it happening in 2026.

I said, "That sounds good." I meant it. I wasn't being sarcastic. Optimism is a resource. I don't have it in the quantity I used to, but I understand its value. If you can keep it through February, you're doing better than most.

Here's what I've learned from eighteen years of January declarations: the people who announce their goals the loudest in the first week are usually the ones who've stopped talking about them by the fourth. Not because they're weak. Because the work was still there waiting. The inbox doesn't care that it's your year.

My coworker is a good person. Smart, capable, genuinely motivated for about three weeks out of every fifty-two. That three weeks has value. It just rarely survives contact with the Q1 planning cycle.

I watched them walk back to their desk. They were already opening their laptop with the specific urgency of someone who has decided things will be different.

I've had that feeling. I remember what it's like to believe a calendar change is a clean slate.

I hope it lasts longer than March this time. I genuinely do.
