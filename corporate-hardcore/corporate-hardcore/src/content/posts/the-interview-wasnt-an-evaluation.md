It was question seventeen of forty-seven.

"What are your development goals for the upcoming year?"

I stared at it for a while. I'd already answered sixteen questions, each one a slightly different version of the same question: *Why should we keep you?*

I typed "remain employed." HR sent it back. Not aspirational enough.

So I changed it to "pursue growth opportunities aligned with organizational objectives." Same goal. Better packaging.

That's the self-assessment. You're not describing what you did. You're justifying why it mattered. And you're doing it inside a form designed for someone whose job is nothing like yours, with a character limit that forces you to compress eighteen months of invisible work into two sentences and a bullet point.

I kept the email system running. Migrated forty thousand accounts without downtime. No one sent a thank you, because no one noticed. That's the accomplishment. Invisibility. When infrastructure works, it disappears. And when it disappears, it stops being visible in a self-assessment.

I've been doing this for eighteen years. I know what the form is for. It's not for the manager. The manager already knows what they're going to write. The form is for the record. It creates the paper trail that says the process happened. It turns the outcome into something that looks like it was earned.

The interview wasn't an evaluation. It was confirmation they'd already decided.

That's not cynicism. That's pattern recognition. Forty-seven questions. Every year, slightly different fonts. Same question.

*Why should we keep you?*

I always answer. I always hit submit. And every year, I think about the people who stopped answering honestly, and what happened to them.
