The all-hands was mandatory.

The CEO opened with the revenue slide. Q4 up twenty-three percent. Best year in a decade. He paused to let that land, and a few people clapped, and then he moved to the next slide, which was about belt-tightening.

I've been in enough of these meetings to know that the revenue slide and the cuts slide existing in the same presentation isn't a contradiction. It's a choice. Margin is a decision. When a company has a record year and announces cost reductions in the same all-hands, they're not struggling. They're optimizing. Those are different things, and the language matters.

He said "leaner operations" four times. He said we need to "do more with less," which is a phrase I've heard so many times it no longer has texture. He thanked everyone for their resilience.

I did the math on the walk back to my desk.

Two headcount eliminated from my team. Zero backfills approved. Workload unchanged. Deadline unchanged. Q4 revenue up twenty-three percent.

The math says the company had a record year partly because my team absorbed work that used to belong to more people. The math also says the response to that record year is to continue absorbing. Possibly more.

"Do more with less" is accurate. What it leaves out is that there's a load-bearing threshold, a point where "more" stops being possible because "less" has become the actual constraint. Nobody at the all-hands mentioned the threshold.

I ate the free lunch they provided. It was good. Catering was not on the discretionary spending list, apparently.

The afternoon was normal. Forty-three tickets in the queue. Three people to work them.

We're operating leaner.
