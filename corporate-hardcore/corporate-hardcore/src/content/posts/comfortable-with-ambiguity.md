Got one interview out of twelve applications.

Video call. The hiring manager was thirty-two. I know because his LinkedIn was visible on my second monitor while we talked. He had the specific energy of someone who has been told they're a high performer and believes it. I recognized the energy. I had it once.

He asked the standard questions. Where do you see yourself in five years. What's your approach to ambiguity and fast-paced environments. Can you describe a time you drove cross-functional alignment. I've been answering these questions since before he was in the workforce. I answered them honestly, which was probably the mistake.

Honest answers to interview questions are not what interviews are for. Interviews are for performing the version of yourself that matches the version of the role they've already imagined. I know this. I performed anyway, because performing otherwise felt like work I didn't have energy for.

He asked if I was comfortable with ambiguity.

I've been comfortable with ambiguity for eighteen years. I have managed systems that were never fully documented, inherited projects without context, absorbed the work of people who left without transition plans. I am so comfortable with ambiguity that I have stopped noticing it.

I didn't say that. I said I thrive in dynamic environments and I believe in clear communication as the antidote to uncertainty. He nodded and wrote something down.

The rejection arrived four days later. "We've decided to move forward with a candidate whose experience more closely aligns with our current needs."

They hired someone younger. I know this because the role was reposted six weeks later with the same description and a salary range twenty thousand dollars lower than what I'd made clear I was looking for.

The interview wasn't an evaluation. It was confirmation they'd already decided.

I've used that line before. It turns out it works in both directions.
