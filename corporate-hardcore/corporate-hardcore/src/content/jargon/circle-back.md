Chuck Morrison heard "circle back" for the first time in 2009. His manager used it to close a conversation about headcount that had been running for six weeks. The phrase landed with a kind of finality that felt, at the time, like progress.

He has since heard it approximately eleven thousand times. His estimate is conservative.

The phrase belongs to a specific category of corporate language: words that create the sensation of movement while preventing it. Like "alignment" (which means consensus has not been reached) or "bandwidth" (which means no), "circle back" performs decisiveness without requiring it. It closes a conversational loop while opening a temporal one that may never be closed.

The geometry is apt, if unintentional. A circle has no end. That is the point.
