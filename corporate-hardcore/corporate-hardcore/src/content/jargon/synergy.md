Chuck Morrison first encountered "synergy" in 2009, in a company-wide email from a vice president who had been in the role for eleven days.

The email announced the consolidation of two regional operations teams under a single director. It described the move as creating "meaningful synergies across our operational footprint." Four people were laid off the following Friday. The word "layoff" did not appear in any subsequent communication.

He has since watched the word do the same work approximately thirty times.

This is what "synergy" is for. It is not a description of what is happening. It is a description of how leadership would like the audience to feel about what is happening. The word performs optimism so that the announcement does not have to perform honesty.

The irony is structural. "Synergy" implies addition — two things producing more together than apart. Its deployment in corporate settings almost exclusively describes subtraction — the removal of redundancy, the elimination of overlap, the reduction of a workforce to its minimum viable size. Using an additive word to describe a subtractive event is not an accident. It is the point.

After eighteen years, Chuck has a reliable heuristic: the more times the word "synergy" appears in an all-hands deck, the fewer people will be in the room the following quarter.
